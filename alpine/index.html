Reflections, Adventures & Stories from the Trails

Do You Know Me?

I'm Yasoob Kamili, a curious developer with a screwdriver in one hand and a trekking pole in the other. Whether I’m exploring alpine lakes and hidden meadows or diving deep into the internals of code and hardware, I live for the thrill of discovery. Peaks excite me—both in the mountains and in technology. I like to open almost everything, from devices to ideas, just to see how they work and how I can make them better. Sometimes, though, things don’t work after I open them—but that’s part of the fun. This blog is where I share my climbs, my code, and everything in between.

Can I Walk on Mountains?

I’ve always been drawn to mountains, even as a child. There’s something about their towering presence that captured my imagination. As I grew, that fascination turned into a passion for trekking. So, can I walk on mountains? Yes—and I do, every chance I get. For me, mountains aren’t just landscapes; they’re challenges, teachers, and quiet companions. I’ve wandered through alpine lakes, traced meadows kissed by clouds, and stood beneath peaks that made me feel small in the best way. Trekking is where I find clarity—it’s where my questions echo back with unexpected answers. Every trail I take is a step closer to understanding not just the terrain, but myself.

Tales from The Trails

Dumail, The First Trail

11 July 2021
The trek begins from Naranag village, situated at around 2277 meters above sea level. A few hundred meters into the trail, trekkers pass by ancient Hindu temples, offering a quick historical stop. After approximately 4 km and 2 hours of trekking, Dumail is reached at ~2400 m ASL. The trail up to Dumail features minimal elevation gain and is rated as easy. The name “Dumail” is derived from “Du” (two) and “Mail” (join), marking the confluence of two rivers—Marchoi Nala and another stream originating from Gangbal Lake. These form the Wangath River. A distinctive wooden bridge at Dumail marks the crossing point to the next section of the trail. From Dumail, the trail transitions into a moderate gradient ascent through alpine meadows for the next 5 km. This section involves a steady climb with scenic views of the surrounding forested ridgelines and open pastures. After approximately 4 additional hours of trekking, the Harmukh peaks come into view, signaling proximity to Marchoi Valley, which sits at an altitude of about 2850 m ASL.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 19 kms

Rainuer, The Endless Meadow

11 June 2023
The trek begins from Hirpora village in Shopian at ~2385 meters above sea level. Trekking in this area usually requires prior permission due to its proximity to sensitive zones and the Hirpora Wildlife Sanctuary. At around 600 meters into the trail, trekkers reach Zrado, followed by a gradual ascent to Baliwal. The trail transitions from forested sections of Shahkoot to more open terrain, and at approximately 6.5 km from the trailhead, the Tsurgul Pass is reached. This pass offers the view to Badgam's Shein Meuin Peak and first major alpine vista, opening up into expansive meadows and panoramic views of the Pir Panjal range, including prominent peaks like Brahmasakli(of Kulgam), Dhakyar(of Shopian), Sunder Top (of Banihal) and more. After crossing the pass, a further 3.5 km leads to the final destination—Rainuer Meadows—at ~3185 m ASL. The meadows are vast, lush, and relatively less explored, believed to be the second-largest alpine meadow in Kashmir. The terrain is grassy, interspersed with boulders and occasional seasonal streams. Although the standard route is a circuit trek (Hirpora → Rainuer → Dubjan Bridge), the group had to return via the same route due to an injury to one member. During the descent, the group was warmly hosted by local nomads at a hut near the trail. Around 15 members of the team were served noon chai, roti, and fresh homemade butter. Despite offering to pay, the nomads politely refused any compensation, showcasing typical high-altitude hospitality.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 19 kms

Mahadev Peak Basecamp: Lidwas

25 June 2023

Route Overview

Originally intended as a one-day summit attempt to Mahadev Peak (3966 m ASL), the plan had to be modified due to road closures in Srinagar caused by a cycle race, resulting in a two-hour delay. The team initiated the trek from Scholar’s School, Dhara (2030 m ASL) without any breaks. After the initial stretch, the trail passed Padshah Pal, followed by an approach toward Bobjan, a section known for high bear activity and considered one of the more dangerous segments of the route. Several members reported past bear encounters here. Following multiple stream crossings, the trail entered Wovur Gul, a steep and energy-intensive climb. After gaining elevation in Wovur Gul, Lidwas became visible at a distance. It took about an hour from this point to reach Lidwas.

Lidwas & Above

Lidwas serves as a seasonal settlement for nomadic communities during the six months of summer. The team reached Lidwas around 1:30 PM. Out of 20 members, 5 decided to push further above Lidwas for better vantage points of Mahadev Peak.The group ascended an additional hour, reaching an altitude of approximately 3520 m ASL, which provided unobstructed views of Mahadev Peak and surrounding ridgelines. On the descent back to Lidwas, a wildlife sighting occurred—an animal with a furry tail was initially suspected to be a fox, but was later confirmed via binoculars to be a Himalayan Marmot, a first-time sighting for the group.

Return Journey

While descending, the team retraced the same route. At Bobjan, a group member narrowly avoided stepping on a green snake, marking another notable wildlife encounter. After exiting Bobjan, the trail gradually eased, allowing the group to recover from the more challenging sections.

Trek Grade: Moderate to Difficult
Total Distance: 19.6 kms


Shilsar, My First Alpine Lake

30 July 2023
The Shilsar Lakes trek began from Margan Top (~3700 m ASL), a high-altitude pass connecting Islamabad with Kishtwar, after a 120 km drive from Srinagar. The trail starts to the right of the pass with an initial steep ridge followed by a gradual ascent through alpine terrain. Out of the four Shilsar lakes, only two were visited on this route as the remaining are more accessible from a different approach. Shilsar 1 (~3900 m ASL) was reached around 12:30 PM with an estimated surface area of 6310 sq. meters (season-dependent), followed by Shilsar 2 (~3920 m ASL) after a 20-minute trek where the team had lunch during rain and hail. The lakes marked the trekker’s first alpine lake experience, with distant views of Nun and Kun peaks visible from Margan Top. The descent concluded at 4:00 PM, with tea at a local dhaba owned by Majid, and departure from Margan Top at 5:30 PM.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate
Total Distance: 11 kms

Trails through The Arshan Valley

20 August 2023
The trek to Arshan Lake began after a ~110 km drive from Srinagar to Sinthan Top, a high-altitude pass in South Kashmir. From there, the trail led into Arshan Valley, and after a few hours of trekking, the group reached Arshan Sar (also known as Drinyan Sar). The route then continued via a steep, rocky ascent to Drinyansar Pass (~4170 m ASL), which offers panoramic views of Zednag, Nehnag, and the Famber Valley. A sharp descent from the pass led to Zednag followed by Nehnag, a distinctive kidney-shaped alpine lake. I further explored the region by ascending to Hoksar 1 before descending back to Zednag. The group then retraced the trail back over Drinyansar Pass and reached Sinthan Top by sunset, completing a high-altitude circuit through a rugged and scenic section of Sinthan.

Trek Grade: Moderate to Difficult
Total Distance: 14 kms

Margan Lakes: Choharnag & Khellannag

03 September 2023
The Choharnag trek began with a 120 km drive from Srinagar to Margan Top, after which the trail started towards the left of the pass. A steep ridge was crossed within the first hour, and after approximately 4 km of trekking, the group reached Choharnag 1, part of a cluster of four alpine lakes collectively named Choharnag (meaning "four springs"). Known as one of the easiest alpine lake treks in the region, the route continued to Choharnag 2, the largest of the four, followed by Choharnag 3, considered the most scenic. The final lake, Choharnag 4, was reached before ascending a steep 200-meter ridge to Khellan Top (~4300 m ASL), a vantage point offering expansive views of Khellan Nag Lake, whose deep blue color was attributed to mineral content and light angle. From this high point, Shilsar 1 and 2 were also visible through binoculars, previously visited on an earlier trek. Lakes Choharnag 1, 2, and 3 were clearly visible from the ridge. After lunch at Khellan Top, the group descended back to Margan Top and concluded the day with tea at Majid’s dhaba, also visited during the Shilsar trek.

Trek Grade: Easy to Moderate(for Choharnag) & Moderate to Difficult (for Khellannag)
Total Distance: 18 kms

Sheesh Nag

15 October 2023
The Sheeshnag trek began after a 90 km drive from Srinagar to Chandanwari (~2740 m ASL), though the journey was delayed due to two flat tyres—resolved with help from a local Tavera driver who provided a spare. After tea and pakoras in Pahalgam, the group began the trek from Chandanwari. The trail, fully concretized by BRO as part of the Amarnath Yatra route, was easy to follow and allowed for a fast pace. Two members of the group quickly ascended to Pissu Top, rejoining another trekking group encountered earlier. The trail then passed Zojibal, home to an Army camp and BRO quarters, where mild snowfall began. These two trekkers reached Sheeshnag Lake (~3600 m ASL) around 12:30 PM after covering 9 km; snowfall intensified, reducing visibility to 100–200 feet. The rest of the group arrived at 1:40 PM. After lunch, the sky cleared, revealing Sheeshnag Lake surrounded by fresh snow-covered peaks. The group began descending at 3 PM and reached Chandanwari before departing back to Srinagar.

Trek Grade: Easy
Total Distance: 18 kms
Total Time on Trail: 7 hrs

Panzkoot Waterfall

05 May 2024
The Panzkoot trek began with a ~70 km drive from Srinagar to Halan village in Manzgam, Kulgam, with necessary permits obtained from the Nihama Army Camp. The trail started from Halan and involved an initial steep ascent through a forest ridge, leading to a vantage point over the Upper Chiranbal Meadows.Some prominant peaks of shopian and kulgam are visible from these meadows, like Brahmasakli and more. After descending into the meadow—an area connected to several other trails such as Hakwas, Zajmarg, Indersar, Astanmarg, and Chirsar—the group passed through a dense forest section to reach the Panzkoot Waterfall by 12:30 PM, where lunch was taken. During the return, they had a tea-coffee mix and interacted with local foragers collecting Guchi Mushrooms(Morel Mushrooms) . We observed some Gucchi before descending back to Halan and returning to Srinagar.

Trek Grade: Easy
Total Distance: 12 kms
